What are co-terminal angles?

Co-terminal angles are angles that have the same initial side and share the terminal sides. The co-terminal angles occupy the standard position, though their values are different. They are on the same sides, in the same quadrant and their vertices are identical. When the angles are moved clockwise or anticlockwise, the terminal sides coincide at the same angle.

Consider $45^\circ$. Its standard position is in the first quadrant because its terminal side is also in the first quadrant. Look at the image as shown in Figure 4.26. On full rotation anticlockwise, $45^\circ$ reaches its terminal side again at $405^\circ$. So, $405^\circ$ coincides with $45^\circ$ in the first quadrant. On full rotation clockwise, $45^\circ$ reaches its terminal side again at $-315^\circ$. $-315^\circ$ coincides with $45^\circ$ in the first quadrant.
Thus $405^\circ$ and $-315^\circ$ are co-terminal angles of $45^\circ$.

The formula to find the co-terminal angles of an angle $\theta$ depends upon whether it is in terms of degrees or radians:
1.  **Degrees:** $\theta \pm 360^\circ n$, where $n$ is an integer.
2.  **Radian:** $\theta \pm 2n\pi$, where $n$ is an integer.

So, $45^\circ, -315^\circ, 405^\circ, -675^\circ, 765^\circ \dots$ are all co-terminal angles. They differ only by a number of complete circles. We can conclude that two angles are said to be co-terminal if the difference between the angles is a multiple of $360^\circ$ (or $2\pi$ if the angle is in terms of radians).

#### Example 1

Find two co-terminal angles of $30^\circ$.

**Solution:**
The given angle is $\theta = 30^\circ$. The formula to find the co-terminal angle is $\theta \pm 360^\circ n$. Then find the first co-terminal angle using $n = 1$.
The corresponding co-terminal angle $= \theta + 360^\circ n$
$= 30^\circ + 360^\circ (1)$
$= 390^\circ$.
To find the second co-terminal angle when $n = -2$ (clockwise).
Then, the corresponding co-terminal angle $= \theta + 360^\circ n$
$= 30^\circ + 360^\circ (-2)$
$= -690^\circ$.

#### Note

From the above explanation, we can find the co-terminal angle(s) of any angle either by adding or subtracting multiples of $360^\circ$ (or $2\pi$) from the given angle. So, we actually do not need to use the co-terminal angles formula to find the co-terminal angles. Instead, we can either add or subtract multiples of $360^\circ$ (or $2\pi$) from the given angle to find its co-terminal angles.

#### Example 2

Find a co-terminal angle of $\frac{\pi}{4}$.

**Solution:**
The given angle is $\theta = \frac{\pi}{4}$, which is in radians.
So, we add or subtract multiples of $2\pi$ from $\frac{\pi}{4}$ to find its co-terminal angles.
Let us subtract $2\pi$ from the given angle as
$\frac{\pi}{4} - 2\pi = \frac{\pi - 8\pi}{4} = -\frac{7\pi}{4}$.
Thus, one of the co-terminal angles of $\frac{\pi}{4}$ is $-\frac{7\pi}{4}$.

What are positive and negative co-terminal angles?
Co-terminal angles can be positive or negative. In one of the above examples, we found that $390^\circ$ and $-690^\circ$ are co-terminal angles of $30^\circ$.
Here, $390^\circ$ is the positive co-terminal angle of $30^\circ$ and $-690^\circ$ is the negative co-terminal angle of $30^\circ$.

#### Note

$\theta \pm 360^\circ n$, where $n$ takes a positive value when the rotation is anticlockwise and takes a negative value when the rotation is clockwise. So, we decide whether to add or subtract multiples of $360^\circ$ (or $2\pi$) to get positive or negative co-terminal angles, respectively.

    c. $\frac{\pi}{6}$ and $\frac{2\pi}{3}$
2.  Find a positive and a negative angle which are co-terminal with angle $55^\circ$.
3.  Find a positive and a negative angle which are co-terminal with angle $\frac{\pi}{3}$.

#### Example 1

Find a reference angle $\theta_R$ of $495^\circ$.

**Solution:**
First let us find the co-terminal angle of $495^\circ$. The co-terminal angle is
$495^\circ - 360^\circ = 135^\circ$.
The terminal side lies in the second quadrant.
Thus, the reference angle is $180^\circ - 135^\circ = 45^\circ$.
Therefore, the reference angle of $495^\circ$ is $45^\circ$.

#### Example 2

Evaluate: $\sin 780^\circ$.

**Solution:**
$780^\circ = 720^\circ + 60^\circ = 2 \times 360^\circ + 60^\circ$.
Therefore, $\sin780^\circ = \sin60^\circ = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$.
