We shall describe two units of measurement of an angle which are most commonly used: **degree measure** and **radian measure**.

### 1. Degree measure

If a rotation from the initial side to terminal side is $\frac{1}{360}^\text{th}$ of a complete revolution, the angle is said to have a measure of one degree, written as $1^\circ$. A degree is divided into 60 minutes, and a minute is divided into 60 seconds. One sixtieth of a degree is called a minute.

| | |
|---|---|
| $1^\circ$ | $60'$ |
| $1'$ | $60''$ |

### 2. Radian measure

There is another unit for measurement of an angle called the **radian measure**. An angle at the center of a circle with radius $r$ which is subtended by an arc of length $r$ unit in a circle is said to have a measure of 1 radian. In Figure 4.5, $OA$ is the initial side and $OB$ is the terminal side of the central angle. The figures show the central angles whose measures are 1 and $-1$ radians, respectively.

We know that the circumference of a circle of radius $r$ unit is $2\pi r$. Thus, one complete revolution of the initial side subtends an angle of $2\pi$ radian. It is well-known that equal arcs of a circle subtend equal angle at the center. Since in a circle of radius $r$, an arc of length $r$ subtends an angle whose measure is 1 radian; an arc of length $l$ will subtend an angle whose measure is $\frac{l}{r}$ radian. Thus, if in a circle of radius $r$, an arc of length $l$ subtends an angle $\theta$ at the center, we have $\frac{l}{r} = \theta$ or $l = r\theta$.

### Relation between degree and radian measure

Since a circle subtends at the central angle whose radian measure is $2\pi$ and its degree measure is $360^\circ$, it follows that $2\pi \text{ radian} = 360^\circ$ or $\pi \text{ radian} = 180^\circ$. The above relation enables us to express a radian measure in terms of degree measure and a degree measure in terms of radian measure. The relation between degree measures and radian measure of some common angles are given in Table 4.1.

#### Table 4.1

| Degree | $30^\circ$ | $45^\circ$ | $60^\circ$ | $90^\circ$ | $180^\circ$ | $270^\circ$ | $360^\circ$ |
|--------|------------|------------|------------|------------|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| Radian | $\frac{\pi}{6}$ | $\frac{\pi}{4}$ | $\frac{\pi}{3}$ | $\frac{\pi}{2}$ | $\pi$ | $\frac{3\pi}{2}$ | $2\pi$ |

#### Note

When an angle is expressed in radian, the word 'radian' is frequently omitted.
Thus, $\pi = 180^\circ$ and $\frac{\pi}{4} = 45^\circ$ are written with the understanding that $\pi$ and $\frac{\pi}{4}$ are radian measures.

Therefore, we can convert degrees to radians using the relations:
Radian measure $= \frac{\pi}{180^\circ} \times \text{degree measure}$
and radians to degrees using the relation
Degree measure $= \frac{180^\circ}{\pi} \times \text{radian measure}$.

#### Example 1

Convert $120^\circ$ into radian.

**Solution:**
We know $180^\circ = \pi$.
$120^\circ = \frac{\pi}{180^\circ} \times 120^\circ = \frac{2}{3}\pi$.

#### Example 2

Convert $\frac{4}{3}\pi$ radian into degree measure.

**Solution:**
$\frac{4}{3}\pi = \frac{180^\circ}{\pi} \times \frac{4}{3}\pi = 240^\circ$. Hence, $\frac{4}{3}\pi$ is $240^\circ$.

#### Example 3

Convert 6 radians into degrees.

**Solution:**
We know that $\pi \text{ radian} = 180^\circ$.
Hence, $6 \text{ radians} = \frac{180^\circ}{\pi} \times 6 \text{ (Use } \pi \approx \frac{22}{7})$
$= \frac{1080 \times 7}{22} = \frac{3780}{11} \text{ degree}$.

#### Example 4

Find the radius of the circle in which a central angle of $30^\circ$ intercepts an arc of length 11 cm ($\pi = \frac{22}{7}$).

**Solution:**
Here, an arc length $l = 11 \text{ cm}$, and $\theta = 30^\circ = \frac{30\pi}{180} = \frac{\pi}{6}$.
Since, $r = \frac{l}{\theta}$, we have radius of the circle,
$r = \frac{11}{\pi/6} = \frac{11 \times 6}{\pi} = \frac{11 \times 6 \times 7}{22} = 21 \text{ cm}$.

#### Example 5

The minute hand of a watch is 1.5 long. How long does its tip move in 40 minutes?

**Solution:**
In 60 minutes, the minute hand of a watch completes one revolution. Therefore, in 40 minutes, the minute hand turns through $\frac{40}{60} = \frac{2}{3}$ of a revolution.
Therefore, $\theta = \frac{2}{3} \times 360^\circ = \frac{4\pi}{3}$.
Hence, the required distance travelled $l$ is calculated as follows:
$l = r\theta = 1.5 \times \frac{4\pi}{3} = 2\pi \text{ units}$.

#### Example 6

If the arcs of the same lengths in two circles subtend angles $65^\circ$ and $210^\circ$ at the center, find the ratio of their radii.

**Solution:**
Let $r_1$ and $r_2$ be the radii of the two circles. It is given that
$\theta_1 = 65^\circ = \frac{\pi}{180} \times 65 = \frac{13\pi}{36}$
$\theta_2 = 210^\circ = \frac{\pi}{180} \times 210 = \frac{7\pi}{6}$
Let $l$ be the length of each of the arcs. Then, $l = r_1\theta_1 = r_2\theta_2$ which gives
$\frac{13\pi}{36} \times r_1 = \frac{7\pi}{6} \times r_2$, i.e., $13\pi r_1 = 42\pi r_2$. Hence, $r_1:r_2 = 42:13$.
